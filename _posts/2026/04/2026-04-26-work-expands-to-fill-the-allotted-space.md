Last year I had a goal of getting down to bug zero, and started a whole series on it. If this is the first you're hearing of this goal, bug zero really means clearing out all doc requests, finishing them. It was similar to the idea of inbox zero.

I actually hit a brief moment where I hit bug zero, and I felt pretty accomplished. As soon as I hit this mark, which only lasted for a day or so before filling back up, my immediate thought was to communicate it. But before I opened my mouth, I quickly realized that if I mentioned it, all the writers who had overflowing bug queues (I know my general group has 1,000+ just waiting for someone to work on them) would ask me to work on their bugs. Alas, since my bug zero moment was so ephemeral, this scenario never really played out. But I realized that reaching bug zero was not something to actively communicate. (Is Tom still needed there? Let's put him to work on all this other stuff that needs a writer….)

I also realized something else lately that has profoundly changed my perspective on the value of the bug zero mentality. The realization is best expressed in this video by a 68-year-old retiree giving advice to those 48 years old:

Ronnie's point that resonated most with me is this: work fills any space you give it. The last couple of weeks I had some late soccer pickups, with my kid having practice until 8:00pm. I usually pick her up on my way home from work, so I decided to just drive all the way in to work and hang out until about 7:30pm, then drive to pick her up on my way home.

This took me out of my usual schedule, which involves driving to the train (Sounder), taking the train to downtown (King Street Station), and then biking the rest of the way to work (only a few miles now to South Lake Union). It turns out that biking to work puts me on a more fixed schedule with the train (the last Sounder train out of the city is at 6:30pm). I'd somehow forgotten the value of a regular schedule.

Staying at work until past 7pm put me in a different mindset at work. I also started doing some stray tasks on the weekends (writing up that weekly report, mostly). In short, I let work start to fill up more of the gaps in my schedule outside of work.

I thought I could push through all those doc tickets and maybe get to bug zero, but guess what — those doc tickets never seem to go away. They fill up endlessly, like a plumbing leak that just keeps pooling up water somewhere.

The idea of using AI to clear out work is a foolish idea: work just expands to fill the newly available space. The amount of work never really diminishes, and if it does in a noticeable way, that probably spells the end of your job.

To use a metaphor, work is like a black vapor that expands into any space it's allowed to move into. Open that door and the black vapor soon fills the entire room. It's like a gas that expands to fill any space homogeneously, spreading its entropy across the universe.

I've decided to avoid letting work expand like this. I need to put more fixed guardrails around my time at work. I stopped driving all the way to work and staying late just because of a late soccer pickup. Instead I use that time to wander around the club facility or do other errands or tasks outside of work, or to sit down at a cafe and write. I like the fixed schedule bookended by the Sounder's timetable and sticking more closely to that schedule, letting it structure my life. I'm also ending this doc bug zero series too. I'll prioritize my doc work and get through as much as I can, but I'm done opening that after-work door to let the black vapor fill my house and life, or filling my head with ideas of finishing everything on my plate. 

There's some other good advice in that youtube video, but honestly this one about work expanding to fill any space you give it is especially true in the AI space. I've written previously about how AI isn't freeing up our time so we can focus on more strategic work. I mean, it's not freeing up my schedule in the sense that I seem to have less work. Instead, the work seems to be expanding, and my long list of tasks is the same length as ever.

Jevons paradox is coming to mind here. If you make a process easier to use, more people will use it. With docs, if you find a way to author content faster, it doesn't mean you'll reduce your workload. Instead, that expanded bandwidth will be consumed by more work that comes to you. Even outside of doc requests, maybe you'll find more issues to address, more errors to fix. 

It's like adding a new lane on a freeway — the new lane (bandwidth) just gets filled up with more cars (work). AI might have cleared up some new lanes for us, but those new lanes aren't empty. They're full of new cars driving down them. Just as cars expand to fill the space of freeways, so too does work expand to fill the space we grant it in our lives.
